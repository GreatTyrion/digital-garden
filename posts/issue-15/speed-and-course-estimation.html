On a foggy night in the English Channel, one of the world's busiest shipping lanes, a vessel slips through the waves with its lights dimmed and its digital voice silenced. This is a "dark ship." By disabling its Automatic Identification System (AIS) transponder, the vessel becomes a ghost, invisible to the standard tracking systems that global commerce and maritime security rely on.

For decades, satellite imagery could prove that a ship was there, but not easily reveal where it was going or how fast it was moving. Now, a breakthrough in multitask deep learning is turning a fundamental "glitch" of satellite physics into a high-precision speedometer.

Section 01

The Problem with "Dark Ships" and AIS Tampering

Most modern vessels are required to broadcast their identity, position, and speed via AIS. In a perfect world, that would create a real-time map of global traffic. In reality, the system is fragile. Signals disappear in crowded ports, coverage gaps stretch across remote waters, and some ships intentionally go silent.

That matters because dark ships are not merely inconvenient to track. They may be connected to piracy, smuggling, illegal fishing, oil spills, or trespassing, turning a technical visibility problem into a maritime safety problem.

"Dark ships are non-cooperative vessels that do not transmit AIS signals. They may be involved in criminal activities such as piracy, smuggling, oil spills, trespassing, or illegal fishing. These ships pose a risk to maritime traffic safety."

When AIS fails, authorities fall back on SAR, a radar-based imaging method that can see through clouds and darkness. But SAR introduces a physics quirk that makes moving ships look as if they are not where they should be.

Section 02

The Doppler Illusion: Why a Ship Is Not Where It Appears to Be

In SAR imagery, the hull of a moving ship can appear physically disconnected from the vertex of its own wake. The image is not broken. The displacement is caused by Doppler shift, because the satellite itself is moving at around 7.4 km/s.

Any target moving perpendicular to the satellite's flight path experiences a frequency shift, and that shift pushes the ship's apparent position in the azimuth direction. To a human observer, the vessel can look like it is sailing beside its own wake rather than leading it.

That visual oddity is exactly what makes speed estimation possible. By measuring the azimuth offset, researchers can work backward toward the ship's true motion.

Section 03

Multitask Deep Learning Solves the 180-Degree Ambiguity

The real bottleneck has never been seeing the ship. It has been automating the interpretation of wakes in noisy, medium-resolution data. Traditional approaches such as Radon transforms can identify linear wake structures, but they often break down when the data becomes messy.

The DTU team sidestepped that limitation with a multitask CNN. Instead of building one model for the ship and another for the wake, they trained a single network to estimate both the ship's course and the Doppler offset at the same time.

That matters because static radar imagery suffers from a stubborn ambiguity: a ship's axis can suggest two opposite directions of travel. Multitask learning lets the model use physical coupling as a hint. If Course Over Ground lies within 0-180 degrees, the offset is positive; if it lies within 180-360 degrees, the offset is negative.

"The multitask loss effectively gives the algorithm a 'hint' that the azimuth offset and COG are coupled. Furthermore, the CNN is forced to share weights to estimate both parameters, which regularizes the model."

In effect, the model is not just detecting shapes. It is learning a compact version of the underlying physics.

Section 04

Precision from Orbit: The Power of 30,000 Images

The study's most striking advantage is scale. Earlier attempts to validate ship velocity from space had only a tiny handful of ground-truth-validated samples. This dataset contains 30,000 AIS-annotated ship images.

Those images come from the Sentinel-1 constellation, which provides wide-coverage C-band SAR imagery at medium resolution, roughly 20 x 22 meters per pixel. Historically, that level of detail was seen as a weakness because wakes could be too pixelated for manual measurement.

The multitask model changed that assumption. It achieved a Speed Over Ground accuracy of 1.11 meters per second, about 2 knots, outperforming single-task models with a mean error of 1.36 m/s.

Section 05

The Blind Spot Shrinks from a Weakness to a Narrow Window

No model escapes the geometry of SAR entirely. The azimuth blind spot appears when a ship sails almost parallel to the satellite's flight path, causing the Doppler offset to nearly vanish.

Older methods become unreliable when the vessel is within roughly 10 to 15 degrees of the azimuth direction. The new approach remains reliable everywhere except within about 2.5 degrees, making it 4 to 6 times more effective for that especially difficult case.

That is not just a benchmark improvement. It is the difference between a laboratory method and a system that begins to feel operational at scale.

Section 06

A New Era for Maritime Surveillance

The ability to automatically extract the velocity of dark ships from abundant, medium-resolution satellite data marks a genuine shift in maritime monitoring. What used to require manual wake interpretation can now be folded into an automated orbital workflow.

By turning the Doppler "glitch" into a high-precision tool, this system makes one thing clear: turning off a transponder is no longer a reliable way to vanish. In a world of AI eyes and orbiting radar, the ocean is becoming harder to disappear into.
